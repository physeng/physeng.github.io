TORQUE (τ)

In Topic 2, forces were acted upon objects, which were simplified to point masses, assuming all forces acted upon the center of masses of the objects. When a force does not act on the center of mass, the object will accelerate as well as rotate, the rotation effect called torque.

τ = F x r

τ: torque about a pivot point
F: force on the object
r: distance from the object to a pivot point



EQUILIBRIUM

When considering torques as scalars instead of vectors, which will be done throughout this unit, it is important to consider torque in the clockwise and counterclockwise directions. As an example, clockwise can be considered the positive direction and counterclockwise the negative direction. With this, an object is balanced when the sum of the torques is zero. Taking this one step further, an object is at equilibrium if both the forces and torques are balanced out.

Forces:
Upwards = 15 N

Downwards = 10 N + 5 N = 15 N

Torques (about A):
Clockwise = 5 N x 4 m = 20 Nm

Counterclockwise = 10 N x 2 m = 20 Nm

Torques (about B):
Clockwise = 5 N x 8 m + 10 N x 2 m = 60 Nm

Counterclockwise = 15 N x 4 m = 60 Nm

From the example, another condition for equilibrium is that the torques about any point on the object sum to zero.

Often times, forces acted are acted on the object at an angle. In these cases, the components perpendicular to the object must be determined through trigonometry and used when calculating torque. The parallel components do not impact the torque as they pass through the pivot and do not have a turning effect.

Bridges

Calculate the forces on each of the supports.

Forces:
Upwards = FA + FB

Downwards = 5 N + 10 N = 15 N

Torques (about A):
Clockwise = 5 N x 0.2 m + 10 N x 0.4 m = 5 Nm

Counterclockwise = FB x 0.8 m

FA + FB = 15 Nm

Hanging signs

In a hanging sign example, following the diagram, the tension of the wire and the weight of the sign are balanced by a normal force from the wall, R, and a reaction force from the wall, F, respectively.

For example, if the sign has a mass 50 kg, the bar a mass 10 kg, the bar a length 3 m, and the wire 50 cm from the end forming an angle of 45°, and calculating the unknown forces,

    Torques (about the wall):
  • Clockwise = T x sin 45° x 2.5 m
  • Counterclockwise = 600 N x 1.5 m
T x sin 45° x 2.5 m = 600 N x 1.5 m

T = 509 N

Since the horizontal forces must be balanced,

    Horizontal forces:
  • Left = R
  • Right = T x cos 45° = (509 N) x cos 45° = 360 N

R = 360 N

Since the vertical forces must be balanced,

    Vertical forces:
  • Upwards = T x sin 45° + F
  • Downwards = 600 N
(509 N) x sin 45° + F = 600 N

F = 240 N

Leaning ladders

In a leaning ladder example, there are normal forces from the wall and ground to balance out the friction of the ladder on the ground and the weight on the ladder, respectively.

For example, if the ladder with mass 20 kg has a length of 5 m with its bottom 3 m away from the wall, and calculating the friction between the ground and the bottom of the ladder,

Since the vertical forces must be balanced,

    Vertical forces:
  • Upwards = Rg
  • Downwards = 200 N

Rg = 200 N

Now calculating the torques about the top of the ladder, since if the torques were calculated about the bottom, the friction would be disregarded,

    Torques (about the top of the ladder):
  • Clockwise = Rg x d = (200 N) x (3 m) = 600 Nm
  • Counterclockwise = F x h + W x (0.5d) = F x (4 m) + (20 kg x 10 m s-2) x (0.5 x 3 m) = F x 4 m + 300 Nm
F x 4 m + 300 Nm = 600 Nm

ANGULAR DISPLACEMENT (θ)

As an object rotates, it sweeps out an angle Δθ, known as the angular displacement of the object and is measured in radians.




ANGULAR VELOCITY (ω)

As an object rotates, the speed of rotation can be described as angular velocity, defined as the angle (angular displacement) swept out per unit time.

ω = Δθ⁄Δt



ANGULAR ACCELERATION (α)

If the torques on an object are unbalanced and the object’s angular velocity is changing, the object has angular acceleration, defined as the rate of change of angular velocity.

α = Δω⁄Δt

These values are the rotational equivalents of displacement, velocity, and acceleration, respectively.




CONSTANT ANGULAR ACCELERATION EQUATIONS

If the angular acceleration is constant, we can use the rotational equivalents to the suvat equations in the exact same way (refer to B.1 in the data booklet).

For example, calculating the final angular velocity for a body rotating at 10 rad s-1 and accelerating at a uniform rate of 2 rad s-2 for 5 seconds,

ωf = ωi + αt
     = (10 rad s-1) + (2 rad s-2)(5 s)
     = 20 rad s-1



GRAPHICAL REPRESENTATIONS OF ANGULAR MOTION

As with linear motion, angular motion can be represented graphically in the same ways.

Similar to linear motion, the three graphs are all connected through fundamental calculus. The gradient of angular displacement gives angular velocity and the gradient of angular velocity gives angular acceleration. On the other hand, the area under the curve of angular acceleration gives angular velocity and the area under the curve of angular velocity gives angular displacement.




ANGULAR MOTION IN CIRCULAR MOTION

Circular motion can be split into two components: a component perpendicular to the circumference and a component tangential to it. When an object moves in a circular path with constant velocity, it only has centripetal acceleration and no tangential acceleration. However, when an unbalanced torque acts on the object, there will be both a centripetal and a tangential acceleration. The tangential acceleration can be calculated with the formula

αt = αr

αt: tangential acceleration of the object
α: angular acceleration of the object

r: distance from the pivot to the object

From the equation, it is known that if an object is rotating with constant angular acceleration:
- All points will have the same instantaneous angular velocity and angular acceleration
- Tangential velocity and tangential acceleration will be greater for points further away from the pivot

NEWTON'S SECOND LAW APPLIED TO ANGULAR MOTION

Newton’s second law that stated F = ma can be applied to torque with the formula

τ = (Σmr2) x α

τ: torque about the pivot
Σm: sum of the masses of all particles in an object
r: distance from the pivot to the particle

α: angular acceleration of the object




MOMENT OF INERTIA (I)

The value Σmr2 is known as the moment of inertia of the object. This simplifies Newton’s second law with angular motion to

τ = Iα

Usually, the value for the moment of inertia is determined through integration, but for this unit, the objects’ moments can all be calculated using formulas (not in the data booklet).

We can think of moment of inertia as being equivalent to mass in linear motion. The greater the moment of inertia, the greater the torque around a point. An object’s moment of inertia depends on its mass distribution and the axis of rotation. In short, an object’s moment of inertia decreases the closer the object’s mass is to its pivot. For example, an object with its mass spread out away from the pivot has a greater moment of inertia than an object with its mass concentrated close to the pivot.




ROTATIONAL KINETIC ENERGY (Ek)

When an object rotates, each particle in the object has kinetic energy, referred to as rotational kinetic energy. This energy is transferred to the object by the tangential force that caused the rotation. The formula for kinetic energy of an object with constant angular velocity is

Ek = 1⁄2Iω2

Ek: rotational kinetic energy of the object
I: moment of inertia of the object

ω: angular velocity of the object

Again, we can see that this equation is similar to the equation of kinetic energy for an object in linear motion.




ROTATIONAL WORK (W)

During angular motion, the total rotational work done by an object is the sum of all the work done during small sections of the motion.

W = τθ

W: work done by the object
τ: torque about the pivot

θ: angular displacement of the object

Again, we can see that this equation is similar to the equation of work done for an object in linear motion.

When a ball rolls down an incline, the weight and normal force act through the ball’s center of mass. However, the friction does not and will cause rotation about the center of the ball and cause the ball to roll. The formula for the velocity of a ball down an incline is

v = √(10gh⁄7)

v: velocity of the ball down the incline

h: height of the ball at the top of the incline




ANGULAR MOMENTUM (L)

The momentum of an object in angular motion, angular momentum, follows a similar relationship to the momentum of an object in linear motion.

L = Iω

L: angular momentum of the object
I: moment of inertia of the object

ω: angular velocity of the object

Similarly, objects in angular motion follow the law of conservation of angular momentum.

INTERNAL ENERGY (U)

The internal energy of a gas is defined as the total kinetic energy of all molecules in the gas. This is, however, only true for ideal gases - gases which consist of perfectly elastic, spherical molecules.

Average KE of a molecule = 3⁄2kT

k: Boltzmann's constant (k = 1.38 x 1023J K-1

T: temperature of the gas

Note that this ratio (3/2) is only true for monatomic gases.

To determine the total kinetic energy of one mole of the gas, this equation must be multiplied by Avogadro’s constant, NA.

Total KE = 3⁄2NAkT

NAk in this equation is equivalent to a constant known as the universal gas constant, R, meaning that this equation can be simplified to

Total KE = 3⁄2RT

Remember that this is for one mole of gas. In order to determine the total kinetic energy or internal energy (U) of a sample of any size, there must be a variable for the number of moles of gas, n.

U = 3⁄2nRT

The change in internal energy of a fixed volume of gas must equal the heat transferred to the gas, assuming no loss of heat to the greater surroundings. Therefore,

Q = ΔU




WORK (W)

Previously, work was defined as force x distance or torque x angular displacement. An alternative way to define force is pressure x area. Substituting this into the first expression yields

W = P x A x Δ

Since A x ΔD simplifies to simply ΔV,

W = PΔV




FIRST LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS

Gases can gain heat, lose heat, get hot, get cold, do work, or have work done on it. The relationship between all of these possibilities can be summarized using the following formula:

Q = ΔU + W

This is one way to represent the first law of thermodynamics, which states that due to the law of conservation of energy, the amount of heat (Q) added to a gas must equal the increase in internal energy (ΔU) and work done by the gas (W).

P-V DIAGRAMS

The thermodynamic transformations that an ideal gas undergoes can be represented using Pressure-Volume (P-V) graph. A movement between two points on the graph signifies a change in the pressure, volume, or both.

An isotherm is defined as a curve on the P-V graph representing all the possible points at a given, constant temperature. This means that all of the points along the blue curve are equal to each other in temperature, and all the points along the red curve are equal to each other in temperature. However, the characteristic temperature of the red curve is higher than that of the blue curve.

With all of this in mind, we can look at many possible types of transformations.




TYPES OF THERMODYNAMIC TRANSFORMATIONS

Isobaric

Isobaric transformations involve constant pressure, with only volume changing. For an isobaric expansion, pressure remains constant whereas volume has increased. This means that work is being done by the gas (since W = PΔV). While it isn’t immediately obvious, the temperature/internal energy has actually increased as well. This is because, in moving from A to B, the gas has switched isotherms. Since Q = ΔU + W, this means heat must have been added to the gas.

Isochoric

Isochoric transformations involve constant volume, with only pressure changing. As a result, no work is done. An increase in pressure at a constant volume signifies that the gas has changed to a isotherm at a higher temperature, meaning the internal energy is increasing as well. Thus, heat has been added.

Isothermal

Isothermal transformations take place within one isotherm. An isothermal expansion is a movement where volume increases, meaning that work is done by the gas. Since this transformation takes place within an isotherm, there is no change in temperature/internal energy. Heat must have been added for the work to be done.

Adiabatic

Adiabatic transformations involve no addition or removal of heat. In an adiabatic expansion, work is still done, meaning volume must somehow increase. It does so by way of the internal energy decreasing (moving to a ‘lower’ isotherm). This allows for the work to be done by the gas to be balanced by the internal energy lost, meaning that the heat added to the gas was 0.

Cyclic processes

These different types of transformations can be combined to form a cyclic process, defined as a series of transformations that ultimately return a gas to its original state.

In this example, the work done by the gas (B → C) is greater than the work done on the gas (D → A), meaning that this cycle results in net work done by the gas. This is the principle behind heat engines.

The theoretical, most efficient cyclic process possible is known as the Carnot cycle.

Instead of the isochoric transformations seen in the initial rectangle shaped process example, the transformations A → B and C → D are adiabatic in nature. The transformations D → A and B → C are isothermal. Since adiabatic processes don’t involve the addition or removal of heat, this is very efficient.




THERMAL EFFICIENCY (η)

Comparing the efficiencies of cyclic processes can be done using an energy flow diagram and values for heat energy:

The thermal efficiency of a general engine can be written in one of the following ways:

η = W⁄QH


η = (QH - QC)⁄QH = 1 - QC⁄QH

For a Carnot cycle, since the transferred heat and temperature (in Kelvin) are directly proportional to each other during the isothermal stages, the efficiency of a Carnot cycle can be written as:

η = 1 - QC⁄QH = 1 - TC⁄TH

ENTROPY (ΔS)

Entropy is a quantity used to represent the spreading out of energy, with the unit JK-1. Most often, changes in entropy are found when heat is added at a specific temperature.

ΔS = Q⁄T


“In all cyclic processes, entropy either stays the same or increases.” This statement is one way to represent the second law of thermodynamics. Two other ways are as follows:

"It is impossible for a heat engine working by means of a cyclic process to convert all absorbed thermal energy to work."

Kelvin-Planck statement of the law

"It is impossible for heat to be transferred from a cold body to a warmer body without work being done."

Clausius statement of the law
